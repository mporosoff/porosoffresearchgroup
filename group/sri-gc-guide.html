Lab Standard Operating Guide

Running the SRI 8610C GC with PeakSimple

A beginner-friendly, step-by-step guide for students analyzing reverse water-gas shift (RWGS) gases — CO₂, CO, H₂, CH₄, C₂H₆, C₂H₄, Ar, N₂, O₂ — on a TCD + FID + Methanizer GC with an automated gas-sampling valve.

Instrument: SRI 8610C (TCD / FID / Methanizer) Software: PeakSimple Sampling: Auto gas-sampling valve off a gas stream Lab: Porosoff Lab — U. Rochester
ℹ How to use this guide Brand-new? Read sections 0–8 in order the first few times. Once you're comfortable, the Quick-Start Cheat Sheet (print it and tape it by the GC) is all you'll need for a routine run. Purple dashed boxes are values your lab fills in once — ask your PI or a senior student. Menu paths look like Edit > Overall.

0Know Your Instrument

Before touching any software, it helps to picture what the GC actually does. A gas chromatograph (GC) takes a tiny, fixed-size puff of your gas mixture, pushes it through long thin tubes called columns, and separates the mixture into its individual gases. Each gas exits the column at its own characteristic time (its retention time) and passes a detector that produces a peak. Bigger peak = more of that gas. PeakSimple is the program that records those peaks and turns them into numbers.

The four parts that matter to you

PartWhat it does (plain English)
Gas-sampling valve (GSV)
(two on this GC)		Instead of injecting liquid with a syringe, a fixed-volume sample loop fills with gas from your reactor stream. Rotating the valve injects that exact volume onto the column. It's automatic and extremely repeatable. The valve is moved by a relay in PeakSimple: relay OFF = LOAD (loop filling), relay ON = INJECT (loop swept onto the column).
TCD detectorThermal Conductivity Detector. A near-universal detector that responds to a gas by how its thermal conductivity differs from the carrier. On this GC it measures the fixed/permanent gases: O₂, N₂, Ar, CO, CH₄, CO₂. Best for higher concentrations (~hundreds of ppm and up); non-destructive. Important limitation: a TCD cannot detect a gas identical to its carrier — because the carrier here is H₂, H₂ is invisible on the TCD.
FID detectorFlame Ionization Detector. Burns hydrocarbons in a tiny hydrogen/air flame and counts the ions. Extremely sensitive to hydrocarbons (CH₄, C₂H₆, C₂H₄ … up to C₅) down to low ppm. By itself it cannot see CO or CO₂.
MethanizerA small heated nickel catalyst with hydrogen, sitting just before the FID. It converts CO and CO₂ into methane on the fly, so the FID can measure CO and CO₂ at low ppm — far more sensitive than the TCD. This is the key to seeing trace CO product and leftover CO₂ in RWGS.
ℹThree channels, one run One injection produces three chromatograms at once — your method records Channel 1 = FID (hydrocarbons), Channel 2 = methanizer (CO & CO₂ measured as methane), and Channel 3 = TCD (permanent gases). You calibrate and read each channel separately: CO and CO₂ are quantified on the methanizer channel for sensitivity, the permanent gases (Ar/O₂, N₂, CO, CO₂…) on the TCD. See the method reference ↓
⚠Your carrier gas is hydrogen Unlike most GCs (which use helium), this instrument uses hydrogen as the carrier gas — and the same H₂ also serves as the TCD reference gas and the FID fuel. The FID's air comes from the GC's internal air compressor. In practice one H₂ source runs almost everything, and the entire flow path carries hydrogen, so ventilation and leak-checking matter (see Safety). One consequence: a TCD is blind to whatever gas is its carrier, so H₂ cannot be measured on the TCD here — it is used in large excess and tracked by the mass-flow controllers or found by difference, not as a GC peak.
⚠A note on water (H₂O) Water is awkward on these columns and detectors and is often not quantified (it may be trapped, vented, or only seen qualitatively). Check with your PI how — or whether — your method reports H₂O before assuming a number is meaningful.

1Safety First

This instrument uses flammable gas, high voltages, and surfaces hot enough to cause serious burns. None of it is dangerous when handled correctly — but read this before your first run and don't skip steps.

⚠Hydrogen is flammable On this GC hydrogen is the carrier gas as well as the TCD reference gas and the FID fuel — so the entire flow path carries H₂. Keep the area well ventilated, leak-check fittings, and never let hydrogen build up in an enclosed space. Light the FID only once it is at temperature with carrier flow established. If you suspect a leak, shut off the hydrogen supply and tell your PI — H₂ is odourless, so rely on the lab's H₂ detectors and leak-checking, not your nose.
⚠Hot surfaces The column oven runs up to 400 °C, and the detectors, methanizer, and valve oven are also hot. Do not touch the oven interior, detector tower, or column fittings until everything has cooled. Let the GC cool fully before opening the oven.
⚠Carrier gas before heat — always Never heat a column without carrier gas flowing through it. Running a hot column dry destroys it. Confirm carrier (hydrogen) pressure/flow is established before the oven and detectors heat up.
ℹGas cylinders Your hydrogen supply feeds the carrier, the TCD reference, and the FID flame, so confirm it has enough pressure for the whole run — at least 500 psig (a hydrogen generator, or a secured/chained cylinder). Air for the FID flame comes from the GC's internal air compressor — no air cylinder needed.

2Daily Startup

Goal: get gases flowing, the GC hot and stable, and the FID flame lit — before you open PeakSimple to run anything. Setpoints live in Your Lab's Settings.

  1. Turn on the hydrogen. Open the H₂ supply (generator or cylinder). On this GC the same H₂ is the carrier, the TCD reference, and the FID fuel. Confirm the regulator/EPC delivery pressure matches your lab's value and that carrier flow is established before any heat is applied.
  2. Power on the GC and the computer. Switch on the 8610C and the attached PC. The GC's internal air compressor starts up to supply the FID air — you should hear it.
  3. Confirm carrier is actually flowing. Check the EPC pressure/flow reading is stable and non-zero before temperatures climb. No carrier = do not heat (see Safety).
  4. Load your method and let every zone reach setpoint. Open your control file (File > Open control file) so the temperatures are correct, then wait for the column oven, valve oven, TCD, FID, and methanizer to all reach their setpoints. This typically takes 15–30 minutes. The methanizer in particular must be hot (often ~380 °C) or CO/CO₂ won't convert to methane.
  5. Light the FID flame. Only once the FID is at temperature with H₂ + air flowing, press your GC's igniter (per the lab step below). Confirm it lit two ways: (1) hold a cool shiny surface — a wrench or mirror — just above the FID exhaust; a fog/condensation film means the flame is burning; (2) the FID baseline in PeakSimple jumps up and then settles. If it doesn't catch, wait a few seconds and try again; repeated failures usually mean the FID isn't hot enough yet or a gas flow is off.
  6. Wait for a flat, stable baseline on all three channels. Watch the FID (Channel 1), methanizer (Channel 2) and TCD (Channel 3) signals. You're ready to run when each baseline is flat and quiet — not drifting or noisy. Drift almost always means a zone is still equilibrating; give it more time.

3Launch & Connect PeakSimple

PeakSimple is the Windows program that talks to the GC, records the chromatograms, and does the math. The very first time (and any time it can't find the GC) you tell it which data system it's connected to.

  1. Double-click the PeakSimple icon on the desktop. The program opens and immediately tries to connect to the GC's data system.
  2. If you see "Acquisition system is not functioning" with a countdown timer, it just means PeakSimple can't talk to the GC yet (USB unplugged, GC off, or wrong port). Click OK to keep working — you can still open and re-process old files offline, but you can't start a live run until it connects.
  3. Open the connection settings: Edit > Overall to reach the Overall Controls screen.
  4. Enter the Board type and port. Set Board type to 302 (your data system — confirm in Your Lab's Settings) and the correct COM port / USB device number. Click OK. PeakSimple establishes communication. You normally only do this once.
  5. Tooltips are your friend. Hover the mouse over almost any button to get a pop-up explaining it. (Turn them off later via the Help menu if you find them distracting.)
✓Connected? When PeakSimple is talking to the GC you'll see the live detector signal moving and the temperature/status zones updating. That live trace at the right edge of the screen is your baseline — wait for it to go flat before running.

5Methods & Control Files

In PeakSimple, a "method" is saved as a control file (a .con file). This single file is the heart of reproducible analysis: it bundles every setting for a run so that you — and the next student — get identical conditions every time.

What's inside a control file

A .con file packages all of these together, so loading one restores the whole method:

PieceWhat it stores
.TEMTemperature program (oven ramps, detector/valve temps)
.EVTEvent table — the timed relay actions, e.g. when the sampling valve injects
.CPTComponent table — your named peaks + retention windows + their calibration curves
.CALCalibration curves for each component
.GRAGraph/display settings
.CHRThe chromatogram data itself (one per channel/run)

Load an existing method

  1. Open your method: File > Open control file, then choose your lab's .con (e.g. RWGS.con). This loads temperatures, the event table, named peaks, and calibrations all at once.
  2. Check the temperatures took. The status zones should now show your method's setpoints. If the GC is warming up, give it time to reach them.

Modify the temperature program

The oven temperature program controls separation. To view or change it:

  1. Right-click anywhere on the chromatogram → Temperature. The Temperature control window opens.
  2. Click Add to open Temperature segment details. Each segment has three numbers: Initial temperature, Hold for (minutes), and Then ramp at (°C/min) Until temperature. Example: start 50 °C, hold 3 min, then ramp 20 °C/min until 250 °C.
  3. Click OK. Add more segments for multi-ramp programs. Click OK to close.
⚠Always save your changes back into the method After editing temperatures, the event table, or components, save with File > Save control file. If you don't, your change is lost the next time the method loads — and the next person gets different conditions. If you're experimenting, Save As a new name (e.g. RWGS_test.con) instead of overwriting the lab's master method.
ℹWhy this matters for RWGS Run-to-run comparisons (conversion, selectivity) are only valid if conditions are identical. Using one locked-down control file is how you guarantee that. Treat the master .con as read-only unless your PI says otherwise.

ℹWhy two CO₂ peaks, and what is "mix"? This method uses two valves: valve G injects the sample, and valve F switches the column plumbing partway through the run (~5.5 min). A gas can then reach the detector by two routes and appear twice — an early peak (e.g. "CO₂ first", ~6.7–7.4 min) from the main column path and a later peak ("CO₂ second", ~15–16 min) from the second / back-flushed path. The "mix" peak is a composite of gases that co-elute (are not separated) on one column path; it is labelled mainly to track the valve step rather than quantified on its own. Which peak is used for each gas is fixed by its retention window — confirm with a known standard which CO₂ peak your lab reports.

  • Component numbering. Make sure no two components share the same peak number.

6Run One Sample (off the gas stream)

Because you sample from a flowing gas stream with a gas-sampling valve, you don't inject by hand. The valve fills a fixed loop, then — at a precise moment during the run — rotates to push that loop onto the column. PeakSimple controls that rotation with a timed event.

Set the injection event (usually already in your method)

Your .con file should already contain this, but here's how to check or build it:

  1. Open the event table: right-click the chromatogram → Events (or Edit > Channels → Events for channel 1).
  2. Click Add. In Event details, select the relay letter assigned to your sampling valve (check the label on the right side panel of the GC — see Your Lab's Settings). Tick the On box (ON = inject).
  3. Set the Event time to when injection should happen — SRI recommends no earlier than 0.10 min (6 s) so the run can initialize.
  4. Add a second event later in the run to turn the relay OFF (untick "On") so the valve returns to LOAD and the next loop fills.
  5. Optionally add a Zero event at time 0 to auto-zero the detector baseline at the start of the run.
ℹReset relays at end of run By default PeakSimple returns all relays to OFF when a run ends (Edit > Overall → "Reset relays at end of run"). That's usually what you want so the loop is refilling between runs.

Start the run

  1. Confirm the baseline is flat and the GC is at temperature.
  2. Make sure the loop is loading: the valve relay should be OFF and your sample gas flowing through the loop.
  3. Name the run / set where it saves (so you can find the data later) per your lab's convention.
  4. Press the Run button (or your lab's start key). The clock starts at 0:00, the event table fires the valve at your set time, and peaks appear on all three channels (FID, methanizer, TCD).
  5. Wait for the run to finish (the full run time). The baseline returns and the chromatogram is complete and saved.
✓Test the valve by hand To check the valve actuates, open View > Relay/pump window and click the relay letter to toggle it (it highlights when ON). You can also hold Ctrl + the relay letter. You should hear/see the valve switch. Return it to OFF (LOAD) before a real run.

7Peak Integration

Integration is how PeakSimple measures the area under each peak — and area is proportional to how much of that gas is present. Good integration = a clean baseline drawn under each peak and the right peaks named. This is where beginners spend the most time, so go slowly.

Step 1 — Name your peaks (the component table)

PeakSimple only reports peaks you've named. For each gas you care about:

  1. Right-click on the peak → Add Component. A horizontal retention window bar appears, centered on the peak.
  2. Adjust the window: drag the small uprights at each end to make it wider/narrower; drag the center of the bar to move it. The window should comfortably bracket the peak.
  3. Double-click under the peak (or right-click → Edit Component) to open Component details. Enter a Peak number, a Peak name (e.g. CO), and a Unit (e.g. ppm or %).
  4. Choose "Show Largest Peak Only" so a stray noise blip in the window isn't mistaken for your gas. Click OK — the peak is now labeled.
  5. Repeat for every gas on that channel, then right-click → Components → Save to store the component table.

Step 2 — Check the automatic integration

PeakSimple integrates automatically using a few parameters. Right-click the chromatogram → Integration to adjust them. The most useful is Area Reject — raise it to ignore tiny noise peaks, lower it (e.g. to 1.0) to catch small real peaks. After changing anything, press Enter (or Edit > Reintegrate) to redraw.

Step 3 — Fix the baseline by hand (when needed)

Open the manual integration toolbar with Edit > Manual Integration. Click a tool, then click the valley/point you want to fix. The ones you'll actually use:

ToolUse it to…
DropSplit two overlapping peaks with a vertical line down to the baseline (most common fix)
BasedRaise the baseline up to the valley between two peaks
NoneMake the baseline ignore a small peak (its area merges into the neighbor)
Lead / Trail skimSkim a small peak off the leading/trailing edge of a big one (a shoulder)
Rubber BandDraw a custom baseline by hand between two points
InhibitStop integrating after a point (ignore everything after)
UndoReverse your manual changes
⚠Be consistent However you integrate your standards is exactly how you must integrate your samples. Inconsistent baselines between calibration and samples is the #1 source of bad numbers. Save your integration choices into the control file.

8Calibration & Calibration Curves

A peak area is just a number until you tell PeakSimple what a known amount of that gas looks like. Calibration means running standards of known concentration and letting PeakSimple build a calibration curve (area vs. concentration). After that, every unknown peak area is automatically converted into a real concentration.

Single-point calibration (the simplest case)

  1. Run (or open) a standard of known concentration that's already named in your component table.
  2. Right-click the peak → Edit [gas name] (e.g. "Edit CO"). If asked whether to copy an existing curve, click No for a fresh one.
  3. In the Recalibration Level window leave Level 1 selected → OK. The blank calibration curve opens.
  4. Type the known amount injected into the top-left cell, then click Accept New. PeakSimple auto-fills the measured peak area and draws the curve.
  5. Click Save, name it (e.g. CO.cal), then Close. (Once named, it auto-saves when you exit.)

Multi-level calibration curve (recommended for RWGS)

Several standards spanning your expected range give a far more trustworthy curve. Repeat the run-and-enter step at each level:

  1. Run/open the Level 1 (lowest) standard. Right-click the peak → Edit → select Level 1 → enter its amount → Accept New.
  2. Run/open the Level 2 standard. Right-click → Edit → select Level 2 → enter its amount in the second row → Accept New. The curve now has two points.
  3. Repeat for Levels 3–7 as needed (up to 7 levels).
  4. With ≥2 levels, click the Method button to pick the curve model: Multiple Line Segments (default), Single line through origin (Ax), or Quadratic through origin. For gas standards, a single line through origin is often appropriate.
  5. Click Statistics to see the fit quality (how well the line matches your points). Then Close.
⚠Levels must be in order You must enter calibration levels in ascending (or descending) order — both the concentration and the area must increase together. If a higher level has a smaller area (or vice-versa), PeakSimple will refuse the point. That usually means a bad run or a mis-typed concentration — re-run it rather than forcing it.
✓Calibrate every gas, on every detector Build a curve for each component you expect (CO₂, CO, CH₄, C₂H₆, C₂H₄, H₂, …) on the detector you'll quantify it with. Calibrations are stored in the component table (.CPT) and travel with your control file.

9Run a Sequence Automatically (AutoQueue)

The Autosampler Queue runs a list of control files back-to-back without you standing there. For an RWGS experiment this is how you take repeated injections off your gas stream over hours — e.g. inject every few minutes to watch conversion change over time — and how you front-load calibration standards before the real samples.

⚠SRI's own caution The AutoQueue is the most powerful and most complex part of PeakSimple. Get comfortable running single samples (Sections 6–8) first. Don't build a long unattended queue until you've done several manual runs successfully.

The idea: one control file per run

Each line in the queue is a .con control file. They must each have a unique name and live in the PeakSimple application directory. Numbering them keeps order obvious: Vial01.con, Vial02.con, …

  1. Open the queue: View > Autosampler.
  2. Add your master method: click Add…, browse to your .con file, and place it in the list.
  3. Make many copies at once: highlight your master and click Add copies. Enter how many runs you want and tick "Increment data filename for each copy" so each injection saves to its own data file (no overwriting). This is the easy way to set up, say, 30 repeat injections.
  4. Fine-tune in the spreadsheet: click Edit all to open a table of every control file's parameters. Edit a single cell, or highlight a column and use Fill to change them all. Click Update control files to save changes.
  5. Save the queue with Save… as a .QUE file so you can reload the whole sequence later with Load….
  6. Start the queue. PeakSimple runs each control file in order, saving a separate chromatogram for each.

Put calibration standards at the front

A common pattern: make the first few runs your calibration standards so the curve is fresh, then let the samples follow.

  1. Open the first control file: File > Open control file.
  2. For each channel, open Post-run actions and tick "Recalibrate at level ___", entering 1 for your Level-1 standard. File > Save control file.
  3. Repeat for the second standard with level 2, third with level 3, etc. (You can also do this from the Edit all spreadsheet.) The remaining sample runs leave recalibration off.
ℹYou must calibrate before queuing The AutoQueue uses calibration curves you've already built (Section 8). Recalibrate-at-level just refreshes those existing curves with fresh standard runs at the start — it doesn't create them from nothing.

initials_reaction_catalyst_temp_YYYY-MM-DD_run##

Example: MP_RWGS_Pt_450C_2026-06-08_run01.chr  →  Marc Porosoff, RWGS over Pt at 450 °C, run 1 of that session.

The rules

RuleWhy
ISO dates — YYYY-MM-DDFiles and folders sort in true date order automatically.
No spaces — use underscores _Spaces and stray dots (e.g. run 5..ASC) break scripts and look messy.
Zero-pad run numbers — run01, run02…They sort correctly and line up with PeakSimple's auto-increment.
One folder per sessionNever mix reactions or dates in a single folder.
Short but meaningfulWho / what / conditions / when / which run — nothing else.

Make PeakSimple actually save there

  1. Create the folders first in Windows Explorer — the student folder and the reaction_date session folder — before you run.
  2. Set the save location: Edit > Overall → fill in Default data file path with that session folder. (This is the Overall controls screen shown in Section 3 — note the "Default data file path" field.)
  3. Set the data-file base name for each channel to your pattern ending in run01. With auto-increment (already on in your method) PeakSimple saves run01, then run02, run03… automatically.
  4. Point the results log (Postrun actions) into the same folder, e.g. results_log.txt.
  5. Save the method with File > Save control file so the path and naming stick.
✎Your lab's choices Data root (off the program drive): D:\GC Data\ · Student folder format: LastName_FirstInitial · Example session: RWGS_YYYY-MM-DD_catalyst_temp
⚠Two PeakSimple gotchas
  • Control files must stay put. Any .con used by the AutoQueue has to live in the PeakSimple application directory — it can't move into a student folder. Keep methods there but named clearly (e.g. RWGS_master.con); only the data goes in the organized folders.
  • Don't move calibration files after linking. .cal and .cpt files are referenced by path inside the method. Pick one permanent location and leave them there, or the links break (see the maintenance checks in The RWGS Method at a Glance).
✓Cleaning up the current mess Build the folder tree first, then copy (don't move/delete yet) existing loose .chr/.asc files into the right student/date folders. Once you've confirmed the copies, drop the originals into an Archive_pre-2026\ folder rather than deleting. Apply the naming convention to new runs going forward — you don't have to rename history.

10Save & Export Data to Excel

Each run's chromatogram is saved automatically as a .CHR file (one per channel). To get the numbers — retention times, areas, and calibrated concentrations — out for analysis, copy them into Excel.

One run's results → Excel

  1. Click the Results button in the toolbar (or View > Results) to open the Results window. It lists each peak: number, name, retention time, area, calibrated result, and units.
  2. Click Copy to put the results table on the clipboard.
  3. Open Excel and Paste. Rows and columns fill in automatically.
  4. To paste the picture of the chromatogram too: right-click the chromatogram → Copy picture, then paste into Excel/Word/PowerPoint.

Many runs → one growing table (best for sequences)

For a sequence of injections you want all results stacked in one place automatically:

  1. In the Results window, click Clear results log once at the start (confirm Yes).
  2. Right-click the chromatogram → Postrun and tick "Add to results log". Now every finished run appends its results to the log automatically.
  3. When done, open the Results window and click Copy results log (note: this is a different button from plain "Copy"). Paste into Excel to get every run in one table. Show results log opens it in Notepad if you prefer.

A whole experiment at once (e.g. 25 injections over 12 hours)

You do not integrate 25 runs by hand. There are two ways to get one combined table:

Automatically, while the experiment runs

This method is already set up for it. When each injection's run ends, its post-run actions fire: PeakSimple integrates the chromatogram against the component table, saves it with an auto-incrementing filename, and appends a row to the results log. So after a 12-hour run you have 25 saved chromatograms and a results log already holding all 25 rows. Open Results → Copy results log and paste into Excel to get the whole experiment in one table.

After the fact (re-processing saved files)

If you need to re-integrate existing data — e.g. after fixing a calibration — use batch reprocessing: View > Autosampler, tick Batch reprocessing mode, click Add multiple, choose the control file, then select all 25 .chr files. PeakSimple runs each one back through that method and (with "add to results log" on) rebuilds the combined table.

ℹOne results log per channel Because this GC records three channels, each detector keeps its own results log (FID, methanizer, TCD). Export the log for each detector you quantify on, or combine them in Excel. Tip: click Clear results log once at the very start so the table holds only this experiment's runs.
✓Name files so you can find them Use clear, dated run names (and the "increment data filename" option in queues) so you can match each chromatogram back to its reactor conditions later. Save into your lab's data folder — see Your Lab's Settings.

11Shutdown & Standby

Most of the time you do not fully shut the GC down — you leave it in standby with carrier still flowing, which is what protects the columns. A full shutdown is only for column changes, maintenance, or extended downtime.

Standby — the normal idle state

Between experiments and overnight, keep the instrument ready and the columns protected:

  1. Leave carrier (H₂) flowing through the columns at all times. Continuous carrier keeps oxygen and moisture out of the column — that is what preserves it. Never leave a column hot with no carrier.
  2. Drop to a standby temperature. Load the lab's standby method (a saved control file) or lower the oven to a modest hold (often ~40–50 °C). A warm column with carrier flowing stays clean and ready.
  3. Turn the FID flame off (shut the FID's H₂ fuel line) so it is not burning unattended — the carrier H₂ keeps flowing on its own separate line.
  4. Leave the sampling valve at LOAD (relays off) between runs.
⚠Leaving hydrogen on unattended — get your PI's policy Because the carrier is hydrogen, an idle GC still has H₂ flowing. Best practice: supply it from a hydrogen generator (it stores almost no gas) rather than a large cylinder, keep the area ventilated, and use an H₂ leak detector if available. On a power failure, shut the hydrogen off at the source — and note that switching the GC off may not stop carrier flow (an EPC can drive flow to maximum when powered down). Whether H₂ is left on overnight is a safety decision your PI must set.

Full shutdown — only when needed

  1. Finish/stop any running sequence and confirm your data is saved.
  2. Turn the FID flame off (shut its H₂ fuel line).
  3. Cool the GC down — load a cool-down/standby method or lower the oven and detector setpoints, and keep carrier flowing the entire time anything is hot.
  4. Only once everything is at or near room temperature, follow the lab's policy for stopping carrier and main power.
  5. Close PeakSimple and log the session if your lab requires it.

⚠Never cut carrier to a hot column If you must stop carrier (e.g. a cylinder change), cool the oven and detectors to near room temperature first. A hot column with no carrier flow can be permanently damaged.

12Troubleshooting

The most common beginner problems and what to check first. If a fix involves gas plumbing, electronics, or you're unsure — stop and ask your PI or call SRI tech support (number below).

SymptomLikely cause & what to try
"Acquisition system is not functioning"PeakSimple can't reach the GC. Check the USB cable, that the GC is powered on, and that Edit > Overall has the right board (302) and COM/USB port.
No peaks at allThe valve may not have injected — check the event table relay letter and time, and that sample gas is actually flowing to the loop. Confirm you're viewing the right channel. For the FID side, make sure the flame is lit.
FID channel is dead/flatFlame likely went out. Confirm H₂ and air, then re-ignite per your lab's procedure. The "fog on a cold wrench" test confirms a lit flame.
Baseline drifting or noisyUsually the GC isn't thermally stable yet — wait for setpoints. Persistent drift/noise can mean a leak, contamination, or a column needing bake-out. Tell your PI.
Peaks appear but aren't namedThe peak fell outside its retention window. Drag the window over it (Section 7) and press Enter. If retention times shifted, check carrier pressure and oven temperature match the method.
Retention times keep shiftingCarrier pressure or oven program changed. Reload the master .con so conditions are identical, and let temperatures stabilize.
Concentrations look wrongCalibration is stale or for a different range. Re-run standards and rebuild/refresh the curve (Section 8). Check the units in the component match your standard.
"Can't enter this calibration level"Levels must be in ascending/descending order for both concentration and area. A point out of order usually means a bad standard run — re-run rather than force it.
Results window is emptyNo components defined, or the channel isn't integrating. Check Edit > Channels → the Integrate box for that channel, and that the component table is loaded.
Extra / ghost peaksCarryover from a previous injection or contamination. Run a blank, check valve timing, and confirm the loop is fully flushed between runs.

13Glossary

TermPlain-English meaning
Chromatogram (.CHR)The signal-vs-time plot a detector produces during a run — the picture full of peaks.
PeakA bump in the chromatogram. One gas = one peak. Its area is proportional to how much of that gas there is.
Retention timeHow long (minutes) a gas takes to travel through the column and reach the detector. Identifies what the peak is.
Retention windowThe bracket you set around a peak so PeakSimple knows "the peak in here is CO."
BaselineThe zero-line under the peaks. Integration draws it; a clean baseline gives accurate areas.
IntegrationMeasuring the area under each peak.
Carrier gasThe gas that continuously pushes your sample through the column. On this GC it is hydrogen, which also serves as the TCD reference and the FID fuel.
EPCElectronic Pressure Control — PeakSimple/GC sets carrier pressure electronically for reproducibility.
Gas-sampling valve (GSV)A valve with a fixed-volume loop that injects a repeatable puff of your gas stream. Moved by a relay.
RelayAn on/off switch PeakSimple controls (named A–H). OFF/ON moves the valve between LOAD and INJECT.
Event table (.EVT)The timed to-do list for a run — e.g. "turn relay A ON at 0.10 min" to inject.
Control file (.con)The full saved method: temperatures + events + components + calibrations bundled together.
Component table (.CPT)Your list of named peaks, their windows, and calibration curves.
Calibration curve (.CAL)The area-vs-concentration relationship built from known standards; converts area → real concentration.
ChannelOne detector's data stream. On your GC: Channel 1 = FID, Channel 2 = methanizer (CO/CO₂ as methane), Channel 3 = TCD.
TCD / FID / MethanizerYour two detectors (universal / hydrocarbon-sensitive) and the catalyst that lets the FID see CO & CO₂ as methane.

★Quick-Start Cheat Sheet

For routine runs once you know the basics. Print this and tape it by the GC — use the 🖨 button in the sidebar to print just this page.

1 · Startup (≈20 min)

  1. H₂ ON (carrier + TCD ref + FID fuel) first; confirm flow.
  2. Power on GC + PC.
  3. Wait for all zones at setpoint.
  4. Light FID flame; confirm (fog test).
  5. Wait for flat baseline.

2 · Open PeakSimple

  1. Launch PeakSimple.
  2. If "not functioning" → check USB / Edit>Overall (board 302, port).
  3. File>Open control file → your RWGS.con.
  4. Confirm temps loaded.

3 · Run a sample

  1. Valve OFF = loop loading from stream.
  2. Baseline flat? Name/locate save file.
  3. Press Run ▶ (clock starts at 0:00).
  4. Event table injects valve (relay ON) at set time.
  5. Let the full run finish.

4 · Integrate & read

  1. Peaks land in their retention windows → named.
  2. Off-window? Drag window, press Enter.
  3. Fix overlaps: Edit>Manual Integration → Drop.
  4. Integrate samples the SAME way as standards.

5 · Calibrate (when needed)

  1. Run standard → right-click peak → Edit [gas].
  2. Pick Level → type known amount → Accept New.
  3. Repeat per level (ascending order!).
  4. Method = curve model; Statistics = fit.
  5. Save .cal; Close.

6 · Sequence (AutoQueue)

  1. View>Autosampler.
  2. Add master .con → Add copies.
  3. Tick Increment data filename.
  4. Standards first via post-run Recalibrate at level.
  5. Save as .QUE → start.

7 · Export to Excel

  1. Results button → Copy → paste in Excel.
  2. Many runs: postrun Add to results log → Copy results log.
  3. Right-click chart → Copy picture for the image.
  4. Save into …\Student\Reaction_date\ — see naming rules.

8 · Standby / Shutdown

  1. Usually standby, not full shutdown.
  2. Keep carrier (H₂) flowing always.
  3. Drop oven to standby temp; FID flame OFF.
  4. Full shutdown: cool first, then per lab H₂ policy.

⚠ Never  ·  Always  ·  Help

  • Never heat a column without carrier (H₂) flowing. Never let H₂ accumulate — keep the area ventilated and leak-checked.
  • Always integrate samples and standards identically. Always let zones stabilize before running.
  • Stuck? Ask your PI/senior student, or SRI Tech Support (310) 214-5092. Relay/valve labels are on the GC's right side panel.
